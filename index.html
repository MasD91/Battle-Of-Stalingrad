The Battle of Stalingrad

  • Background
  • Course of the Battle
  • Conclusion

Background

The Battle of Stalingrad was a major turning point in World War II. Fought between July 1942 and February 1943, it was one of the largest and bloodiest battles in the history of warfare. Stalingrad, now known as Volgograd, was a strategically important city in the Soviet Union, located on the Volga River. The German army sought to capture the city as part of their effort to defeat the Soviet Union and secure control of the oil fields in the Caucasus.

Course of the Battle

The battle was fought between the German 6th Army and elements of the Soviet 62nd and 64th Armies. The fighting was brutal and intense, with control of the city constantly changing hands. Eventually, the Soviets were able to encircle the German army and force their surrender.

Conclusion

The Battle of Stalingrad was a significant turning point in the war. The defeat of the German army marked the end of their advance into the Soviet Union and set the stage for the eventual defeat of the Axis powers in Europe.
